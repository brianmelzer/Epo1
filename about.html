The Maison

Born of a Paris brasserie and a Swedish forge — a house built on one honest material.

Origin · 2014

It began with a borrowed pan

In the kitchen of a small brasserie off the Rue de Bretagne, chef Élodie Marchand cooked on a single, battered carbon-steel skillet that had belonged to her grandmother. It was nothing to look at — dark, scarred, beautiful. But it seared like nothing she could buy new, and it had outlived three ovens and two restaurants.

When a regular — Anders Lindqvist, a metallurgist from a centuries-old Swedish steelworks — asked why the finest kitchens still cooked on such humble steel, the answer became an obsession: why can't the everyday pan be the best one?

Ferveur was their reply. A French eye for proportion and ritual, joined to Swedish steel and the discipline of the forge.

Élodie & Anders
The Atelier

Drawn in Paris. Forged in Sweden.

Every Ferveur piece is designed in our atelier in the Marais — where the curve of a handle, the depth of a wall and the balance in the hand are argued over like a recipe.

The steel itself is pressed and spun in Sweden, from a single sheet of high-carbon steel. No coatings. No non-stick chemistry. Just iron and carbon, heat-treated and then seasoned by hand in oil until the surface turns the deep blue-black of a well-loved pan.

It performs to 1200°F, never warps, and grows more non-stick with every meal. This is cookware that is not finished when it ships — it is only beginning.

The Ritual

Caring for carbon steel

Three habits, and your Ferveur will outlive every other pan in the kitchen.

01

Cook With It

Use it often, and use fat. Every sear builds the seasoning — the dark, naturally non-stick patina that defines great carbon steel.

02

Clean It Warm

Rinse warm, wipe dry, skip the soap when you can. A quick scrub of coarse salt lifts anything stubborn without stripping the surface.

03

Oil & Rest

A thin film of oil over gentle heat before it cools, and your pan is sealed and ready for the next flame.
